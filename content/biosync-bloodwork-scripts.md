### Metabolic Markers

#### Fasting Glucose *
- **What it measures:** Your blood sugar level after not eating for 8-12 hours — a snapshot of how your body manages glucose at rest.
- **Why it matters for shift workers:** Irregular eating windows and overnight shifts disrupt your body's ability to regulate blood sugar. Shift workers frequently show elevated fasting glucose even when they "eat clean" — because timing and cortisol matter as much as food quality.
- **Optimal range:** 75-86 mg/dL (functional optimal). Standard "normal" goes up to 99, but metabolic dysfunction starts brewing well before that.
- **When it's off in shift workers:** Fasting glucose of 90-99 is technically "normal" but often signals early insulin resistance driven by cortisol. You might notice: afternoon energy crashes, waking up hungry at 2 AM, sugar cravings after a night shift. Your body is stuck in glucose-dump mode from chronic stress.

#### Fasting Insulin *
- **What it measures:** How much insulin your pancreas has to produce to keep your blood sugar in range — the effort behind the number.
- **Why it matters for shift workers:** This is the marker most providers skip, and it's the one that matters most. Your fasting glucose can look "fine" while your insulin is working overtime to keep it there. Shift work raises cortisol, cortisol raises blood sugar, and your pancreas compensates by pumping out more insulin. That excess insulin tells your body to store belly fat. This is the cortisol-insulin-belly fat loop.
- **Optimal range:** 2-6 uIU/mL (functional optimal). Standard lab range goes up to 25 — by that point you're already deep into insulin resistance.
- **When it's off in shift workers:** Fasting insulin above 8-10 usually means your body is in fat-storage mode regardless of how well you eat. Classic signs: belly fat that won't budge despite calorie restriction, feeling "puffy," rings tight in the morning, and cravings you can't willpower your way through. Your body isn't broken — it's responding to the insulin signal.

#### HbA1c *
- **What it measures:** Your average blood sugar over the past 2-3 months — the long game, not a single snapshot.
- **Why it matters for shift workers:** Fasting glucose catches a moment; HbA1c catches the pattern. Shift workers often have normal fasting glucose but elevated A1c because their blood sugar spikes unpredictably throughout irregular shifts. It's the hidden metabolic cost of rotating schedules.
- **Optimal range:** 4.8-5.2% (functional optimal). Standard "normal" goes up to 5.6%, and "prediabetic" starts at 5.7% — but metabolic changes are happening well before 5.7%.
- **When it's off in shift workers:** A1c of 5.3-5.6% in a shift worker usually signals blood sugar instability throughout the day — not diabetes risk per se, but a stress-metabolism pattern. You might feel fine some shifts and completely wiped on others. The inconsistency is the clue.

#### Lipid Panel (Total Cholesterol, LDL, HDL, Triglycerides) *
- **What it measures:** Your blood fats — total cholesterol, LDL ("lousy"), HDL ("helpful"), and triglycerides (stored sugar converted to fat).
- **Why it matters for shift workers:** Triglycerides are the most shift-relevant marker here. Elevated triglycerides in a shift worker almost always trace back to blood sugar instability and insulin resistance — not dietary fat. When your body can't use glucose properly, it converts the excess to triglycerides. HDL tends to drop when you're chronically stressed and under-sleeping.
- **Optimal ranges:**
  - Total cholesterol: 180-220 mg/dL (context-dependent)
  - LDL: under 100 mg/dL
  - HDL: above 60 mg/dL (above 70 is ideal for women)
  - Triglycerides: under 80 mg/dL (functional optimal). Standard "normal" goes up to 150 — that's too high.
  - Triglyceride-to-HDL ratio: under 1.5 (this ratio is a better insulin resistance indicator than either number alone)
- **When it's off in shift workers:** High triglycerides (100+) with low HDL (under 50) is the classic shift-worker metabolic pattern. It screams insulin resistance driven by cortisol. You're not eating too much fat — your body is converting excess blood sugar into fat because it can't clear it efficiently.

### Thyroid Markers

#### TSH *
- **What it measures:** Thyroid-stimulating hormone — how hard your brain is signaling your thyroid to work. Higher TSH means your brain is yelling louder because your thyroid isn't keeping up.
- **Why it matters for shift workers:** Chronic cortisol suppresses thyroid function. Your body literally downregulates metabolism when it's stuck in survival mode. Many shift workers are told their thyroid is "fine" because their TSH is within the standard range — but it's not optimal, and they feel every point of that difference.
- **Optimal range:** 1.0-2.0 mIU/L (functional optimal). Standard "normal" goes up to 4.5, but many women feel sluggish above 2.5.
- **When it's off in shift workers:** TSH of 2.5-4.5 in a chronically stressed shift worker often means cortisol is suppressing thyroid output. Symptoms: metabolism feels "slow" despite doing everything right, cold hands and feet, hair thinning, can't lose weight on a deficit. This is why "eat less, move more" backfires — your thyroid has already downshifted.

#### Free T3
- **What it measures:** The active thyroid hormone — the one that actually drives your metabolism. T4 is the storage form; T3 is the gas pedal.
- **Why it matters for shift workers:** Stress and poor sleep impair the conversion of T4 to T3. You can have a "normal" TSH and T4 while your Free T3 is tanked — meaning your body has the raw materials but isn't converting them into usable metabolic fuel.
- **Optimal range:** 3.0-3.5 pg/mL (functional optimal). Standard range starts at 2.0, but below 2.8 most women notice symptoms.
- **When it's off in shift workers:** Low Free T3 with normal TSH is one of the most under-recognized patterns in shift workers. You'll feel it as: persistent fatigue that sleep doesn't fix, stubborn weight, brain fog, needing caffeine just to function. Your metabolism is running on fumes.

#### Free T4
- **What it measures:** The storage form of thyroid hormone — the raw material your body converts into active T3.
- **Why it matters for shift workers:** Provides context for the full thyroid picture. If T4 is adequate but T3 is low, the problem isn't thyroid production — it's conversion, usually impaired by cortisol and inflammation.
- **Optimal range:** 1.1-1.5 ng/dL (functional optimal). Standard range is 0.8-1.8.
- **When it's off in shift workers:** Low T4 suggests the thyroid itself is struggling (possibly from prolonged stress suppression). Normal T4 with low T3 points to a conversion problem — and that's the more common shift-worker pattern.

### Stress & Inflammation Markers

#### Cortisol (AM) *
- **What it measures:** Your primary stress hormone, measured in the morning when it should be at its highest (cortisol follows a natural curve — high in the AM, low at night).
- **Why it matters for shift workers:** This is the centerpiece of the SmithFit approach. Shift work disrupts your cortisol rhythm. Night shift workers often have a flattened or inverted cortisol curve — their cortisol doesn't peak properly in the morning and doesn't drop at night. This single disruption cascades into insulin resistance, thyroid suppression, belly fat storage, cravings, and fatigue.
- **Optimal range:** 10-15 mcg/dL between 7-9 AM (functional optimal). Standard range is 6-23, which is nearly useless because it's so wide.
- **When it's off in shift workers:** Low morning cortisol (under 10) in a shift worker often indicates HPA axis fatigue — your stress response system has been running so hard for so long that it's starting to flatline. You'll feel it as: can't wake up without coffee, afternoon crashes, second wind at 10 PM, exhaustion that sleep doesn't fix. High morning cortisol (above 18) suggests your body is still stuck in overdrive. Note: If you work nights, this number needs context — tell us your shift schedule when you upload so we can interpret accordingly.

#### hs-CRP (High-Sensitivity C-Reactive Protein) *
- **What it measures:** Systemic inflammation — a general marker of how much low-grade inflammation is circulating in your body.
- **Why it matters for shift workers:** Chronic sleep disruption drives inflammation. Period. Nurses who work rotating shifts consistently show higher CRP than day-shift workers, even when everything else is equal. This inflammation contributes to water retention, puffiness, joint aches, and makes fat loss harder because your body prioritizes healing over metabolic efficiency.
- **Optimal range:** Under 0.5 mg/L (functional optimal). Standard "normal" goes up to 3.0, and cardiac risk starts above 3.0 — but metabolic inflammation is causing problems well before that.
- **When it's off in shift workers:** CRP of 1.0-3.0 in a shift worker is almost always driven by sleep disruption and stress, not infection or acute illness. Symptoms: puffiness (especially face and belly), clothes fitting tighter some days than others, joint stiffness, feeling "inflamed." This is your body's alarm system responding to the biological stress of shift work.

#### Homocysteine
- **What it measures:** An amino acid that rises when your body can't properly methylate (a key detox and repair process) — often due to B-vitamin deficiency or genetic variations.
- **Why it matters for shift workers:** Elevated homocysteine increases cardiovascular risk and signals that your body's repair mechanisms are compromised. Shift workers are more likely to have B-vitamin depletion (from stress, poor absorption from irregular eating, and higher metabolic demand), which drives homocysteine up.
- **Optimal range:** Under 8 umol/L (functional optimal). Standard "normal" goes up to 15.
- **When it's off in shift workers:** Homocysteine above 10 in a shift worker usually points to B-vitamin depletion (especially B12, B6, and folate). You might notice: brain fog, mood dips, and fatigue that seems disproportionate to your sleep. It's a fixable problem once you know it's there.

### Nutrient Status

#### Vitamin D (25-OH) *
- **What it measures:** Your vitamin D level — essential for immune function, mood, bone health, energy, and metabolic function.
- **Why it matters for shift workers:** Nurses are almost universally deficient. You work 12-hour shifts indoors under fluorescent lights, and when you're off, you're recovering — not sunbathing. Night-shift nurses are especially depleted. Low vitamin D is linked to fatigue, depression, weakened immunity (you catch every bug on the unit), and impaired fat metabolism.
- **Optimal range:** 50-70 ng/mL (functional optimal). Standard "normal" starts at 30, but most functional practitioners agree that below 50 is suboptimal. Many nurses test in the 15-25 range.
- **When it's off in shift workers:** Vitamin D below 40 in a shift worker is extremely common and contributes to: persistent fatigue, getting sick frequently, low mood (especially in winter or on a night-shift stretch), and slower fat loss. This is one of the easiest and most impactful things to fix.

#### Ferritin *
- **What it measures:** Your iron storage — not just "are you anemic" but how much iron your body has in reserve.
- **Why it matters for shift workers:** Ferritin drops before anemia shows up on standard labs. Many nurses have ferritin levels that are "normal" (above 12) but nowhere near optimal — and they're dragging through every shift wondering why they're so exhausted. Menstruating women are especially vulnerable.
- **Optimal range:** 50-100 ng/mL for women (functional optimal). Standard "normal" starts at 12 — that's the floor before you're clinically anemic. Feeling good requires much more than "not anemic."
- **When it's off in shift workers:** Ferritin of 12-40 is the most underdiagnosed energy issue in female nurses. Your hemoglobin and CBC look "fine," but your iron stores are depleted. Symptoms: exhaustion beyond normal shift fatigue, breathlessness going up stairs, restless legs, poor exercise recovery, ice cravings. This is not "just being tired from work" — it's measurable and fixable.

#### Vitamin B12
- **What it measures:** B12 is essential for energy production, nerve function, and red blood cell formation.
- **Why it matters for shift workers:** Stress burns through B-vitamins faster. Irregular eating patterns and relying on coffee and quick snacks during shifts means you're likely not absorbing enough. B12 deficiency mimics and worsens shift-work fatigue.
- **Optimal range:** 500-800 pg/mL (functional optimal). Standard "normal" starts at 200, but neurological symptoms can appear below 400.
- **When it's off in shift workers:** B12 of 200-400 in a shift worker often shows up as: brain fog that coffee doesn't fix, tingling in hands or feet, mood changes, and fatigue layered on top of already being tired from shifts. Easy to supplement once identified.

#### Magnesium (RBC)
- **What it measures:** Magnesium stored inside your red blood cells — a much more accurate measure than standard serum magnesium, which only shows what's floating in your blood (and is the last place magnesium drops).
- **Why it matters for shift workers:** Magnesium is the calming mineral. Stress depletes it. Caffeine depletes it. Poor sleep depletes it. As a shift worker running on all three, you're likely deficient. Low magnesium contributes to muscle cramps, poor sleep quality, anxiety, constipation, and an inability to "come down" after a shift.
- **Optimal range:** 5.0-6.5 mg/dL (functional optimal). Standard range is 4.2-6.8. You want to be in the upper half.
- **When it's off in shift workers:** RBC magnesium below 5.0 is extremely common in shift workers and explains: can't fall asleep after a shift even though you're exhausted, muscle cramps or twitching, feeling wired but tired, constipation, anxiety that gets worse on work days. Magnesium is one of the first things we optimize in the SmithFit protocol.

### Hormonal Markers (Optional — Recommended for 40+)

#### Estradiol
- **What it measures:** The primary form of estrogen — the hormone that influences fat distribution, mood, bone health, and metabolic rate in women.
- **Why it matters for shift workers:** Perimenopause and menopause change everything metabolically, and shift work accelerates these changes. Irregular shifts disrupt the hormonal axis that governs estrogen production. Declining estrogen shifts fat storage to the belly (even if you've always been pear-shaped), increases insulin resistance, and worsens sleep quality.
- **Optimal range:** Depends on menstrual status and cycle phase. Premenopausal (mid-cycle): 100-400 pg/mL. Postmenopausal: under 30 pg/mL is expected but doesn't mean symptoms are inevitable.
- **When it's off in shift workers:** Fluctuating or declining estradiol in a 40+ shift worker amplifies every other metabolic issue — insulin resistance gets worse, cortisol impact increases, sleep deteriorates further, and belly fat becomes more stubborn. If you're in perimenopause AND working shifts, you're dealing with a double metabolic hit.

#### Progesterone
- **What it measures:** The calming counterbalance to estrogen — essential for sleep quality, anxiety regulation, and cycle regularity.
- **Why it matters for shift workers:** Progesterone is the first hormone to decline in perimenopause, and chronic stress suppresses it further. Low progesterone is a major driver of the "wired but tired" feeling shift workers describe — your body can't calm down.
- **Optimal range:** Depends on cycle phase. Mid-luteal (Day 19-22): 10-25 ng/mL. Postmenopausal: low is expected.
- **When it's off in shift workers:** Low progesterone in a shift worker looks like: terrible sleep even when you have time to sleep, anxiety before shifts, PMS that's gotten worse over the years, spotting or irregular cycles. Stress is stealing the raw materials your body needs to make progesterone.

#### Free & Total Testosterone
- **What it measures:** Not just a "male hormone" — testosterone in women drives energy, motivation, muscle maintenance, and metabolic rate.
- **Why it matters for shift workers:** Chronic stress and poor sleep lower testosterone in women. Low testosterone means: harder to build or maintain muscle, lower motivation, and a metabolism that feels sluggish. This is why some shift workers feel like they're losing muscle despite working out.
- **Optimal range:** Total testosterone: 40-70 ng/dL for women. Free testosterone: 2-5 pg/mL.
- **When it's off in shift workers:** Low testosterone in a female shift worker shows up as: loss of muscle tone despite exercising, low motivation and drive, fatigue that's more "flat" than "sleepy," decreased exercise recovery. Your body is conserving resources instead of building.

#### DHEA-S
- **What it measures:** A precursor hormone made by your adrenal glands — the raw material for both estrogen and testosterone. It's also a marker of adrenal reserve.
- **Why it matters for shift workers:** DHEA-S declines naturally with age but drops faster under chronic stress. Low DHEA-S in a shift worker signals that your adrenal glands are running on empty — they've been producing cortisol for so long that they can't keep up with other hormone production.
- **Optimal range:** Age-dependent, but for women 35-50: 150-350 mcg/dL (functional optimal).
- **When it's off in shift workers:** Low DHEA-S combined with low or flat cortisol is the "burned out" pattern — your adrenals have been overworked by years of shift stress. You feel old before your time: low energy, low drive, low resilience. The good news is this responds well to stress regulation (exactly what we do in SmithFit).
